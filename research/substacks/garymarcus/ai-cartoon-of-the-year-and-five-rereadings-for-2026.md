And, bonus, to help prepare you to think critically about AI 2026, in an era in which everyone is wondering whether generative AI will [turn out to do be a dud](https://cacm.acm.org/blogcacm/what-if-generative-ai-turned-out-to-be-a-dud/), here are five essays from 2025 worth reading or rereading: 

  1. [Why LLMs just can’t stop hallucinating](https://open.substack.com/pub/garymarcus/p/why-do-large-language-models-hallucinate?utm_campaign=post-expanded-share&utm_medium=web).

  2. [Generalization is — and always has been — the vital question](https://open.substack.com/pub/garymarcus/p/a-knockout-blow-for-llms?r=8tdk6&utm_medium=ios&shareImageVariant=overlay). How Apple’s reasoning paper brought that back to the fore.

  3. [LLMs are not like you and me—and never will be](https://open.substack.com/pub/garymarcus/p/llms-are-not-like-you-and-meand-never?utm_campaign=post-expanded-share&utm_medium=web).

  4. [What world models are, and why we desperately need them](https://open.substack.com/pub/garymarcus/p/generative-ais-crippling-and-widespread?utm_campaign=post-expanded-share&utm_medium=web). 

  5. [Neurosymbolic AI will displace pure LLMs over time](https://open.substack.com/pub/garymarcus/p/how-o3-and-grok-4-accidentally-vindicated?utm_campaign=post-expanded-share&utm_medium=web); here’s why.




If the above analysis is correct, the dominance of LLMs may soon start to decline — and we may start seeing newer, more sound approaches start to emerge.
