Today I break long radio silence to deliver some phenomenal news. Two of the people who I eat lunch with every week--my MIT CSAIL colleagues [Silvio Micali](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silvio_Micali) and [Shafi Goldwasser](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shafi_Goldwasser)--have [won a well-deserved Turing Award](http://www.acm.org/news/featured/awards/turing-award-2012), for their fundamental contributions to cryptography from the 1980s till today. (I see that Lance [just now beat me](http://blog.computationalcomplexity.org/2013/03/turing-award-to-shafi-and-silvio.html) to a blog post about this. Dammit, Lance!)

I won't have to tell many readers of this blog that the names Goldwasser and Micali--or more often, the initials "G" and "M"--are as ubiquitous as Alice and Bob in modern cryptography, from the GGM construction of pseudorandom functions ([discussed before](https://scottaaronson.blog/?p=403) on this blog), to the classic [GMR paper](http://groups.csail.mit.edu/cis/pubs/shafi/1989-siamjc.pdf) that introduced the world to interactive proofs. Besides that, Shafi and Silvio are known as two of the more opinionated and colorful characters of theoretical computer science--and as I learned last week, Silvio is also an _awesome_ party host, who has perfect taste in sushi (as well as furniture and many other things).

I wish I could go on right now talking about Shafi and Silvio--and even more, that I could join the celebration that will happen at MIT this afternoon. But I'm about to board a flight to LAX, to attend the [60th birthday symposium](http://preskill60.caltech.edu/) of longtime friend, extraordinary physicist, and sometime _Shtetl-Optimized_ commenter [John Preskill](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Preskill). (I'll also be bringing you coverage of that symposium, including slides from my talk there on hidden variables.) So, leave your congratulations, etc. in the comments section, and I'll see them when I land!
