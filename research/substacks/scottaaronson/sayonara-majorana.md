Many of you have surely [already](https://www.wired.com/story/microsoft-retracts-disputed-quantum-computing-paper/) [seen](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-021-00612-z) the news that the Kouwenhoven group in Delft--which in 2018 published a paper in _Nature_ claiming to have detected [Majorana particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Majorana_fermion), a type of nonabelian [anyon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anyon)--have [retracted the paper](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-021-03373-x?utm_medium=affiliate&utm_source=commission_junction&utm_campaign=3_nsn6445_deeplink_PID100095187&utm_content=deeplink) and apologized for "insufficient scientific rigour." This work was considered one of the linchpins of Microsoft's experimental effort toward building topological quantum computers.

Like most quantum computing theorists, I guess, I'm thrilled if Majorana particles can be created using existing technology, I'm sad if they can't be, but I don't have any special investment in or knowledge of the topic, beyond what I read in the news or hear from colleagues. Certainly Majorana particles seem neither necessary nor sufficient for building a scalable quantum computer, although they'd be a step forward for the topological approach to QC.

The purpose of this post is to invite _informed scientific discussion_ of the relevant issues--first and foremost so that I can learn something, and second so that my readers can! I'd be especially interested to understand:

  1. Weren't there, like, several _other_ claims to have produced Majoranas? What of those then?
  2. If, today, no one has convincingly demonstrated the existence of Majoranas, then do people think it more likely that they were produced but not detected, or that they weren't even produced?
  3. How credible are the explanations as to what went wrong?
  4. Are there any broader implications for the prospects of topological QC, or Microsoft's path to topological QC, or was this just an isolated mistake?
