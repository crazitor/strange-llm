For years, I was forced to endure life with my nose up against the glass of the [Annual _Edge_ Question](http://edge.org/contributors/what-do-you-consider-the-most-interesting-recent-scientific-news-what-makes-it). [What are you optimistic about?](http://edge.org/annual-question/what-are-you-optimistic-about) Ooh! ooh! Call on me! I'm optimistic about someday being able to _prove_ my pessimistic beliefs (like P≠NP). [How is the Internet changing the way you think?](http://edge.org/annual-question/how-is-the-internet-changing-the-way-you-think) Ooh, ooh! I know! Google and MathOverflow are saving me from having to think at all! So then why are they only asking Steven Pinker, Freeman Dyson, Richard Dawkins, David Deutsch, some random other people like that?

But all that has changed. This year, I was invited to participate in _Edge_ for the first time. So, OK, here's the question:

**What do you consider the most interesting recent [scientific] news? What makes it important?**

[My response is here](http://edge.org/response-detail/26637). I wasn't in love with the question, because of what I saw as an inherent ambiguity in it: the news that's most interesting to _me_ , that I have a comparative advantage in talking about, and that people probably want to hear me talk about (e.g., progress in quantum computing), is not necessarily what I'd regard as the most important in any objective sense (e.g., climate change). So, I decided to write my answer precisely about my internal tension in what I should consider most interesting: should it be the recent progress by John Martinis and others toward building a quantum computer? Or should it be the melting glaciers, or something else that I'm confident will affect the future of the world? Or possibly the mainstream attention now being paid to the AI-risk movement? But if I really want to nerd out, then why not Babai's graph isomorphism algorithm? Or if I _actually_ want to be honest about what excited me, then why not the superquadratic separations between classical and quantum query complexities for a total Boolean function, by Ambainis et al. and my student Shalev Ben-David? On the other hand, how can I justify even caring about such things while the glaciers are melting?

So, yeah, my response tries to meditate on all those things. My original title was "How nerdy do you want it?," but John Brockman of Edge had me change it to something blander ("How widely should we draw the circle?"), and made a bunch of other changes from my usual style. Initially I chafed at having an _editor_ for what basically amounted to a blog post; on the other hand, I'm sure I would've gotten in trouble much less often on this blog had I had someone to filter my words for me.

Anyway, of course I wasn't the only person to write about the climate crisis. [Robert Trivers](http://edge.org/response-detail/26578), [Laurence Smith](http://edge.org/response-detail/26685), and [Milford Wolpoff](http://edge.org/response-detail/26591) all wrote about it as well (Trivers most chillingly and concisely), while [Max Tegmark](http://edge.org/response-detail/26687) wrote about the mainstreaming of AI risk. [John Naughton](http://edge.org/response-detail/26712) even wrote about Babai's graph isomorphism breakthrough (though he seems unaware that the existing GI algorithms were already extremely fast in practice, and therefore makes misleading claims about the new algorithm's practical applications). Unsurprisingly, no one else wrote about breakthroughs in quantum query complexity: you'll need to go to _my_ essay for that! A bit more surprisingly, no one besides me wrote about progress in quantum computing at all (if we don't count the loophole-free Bell test).

Anyway, on reflection, **2015 actually was a pretty awesome year for science** , no matter how nerdy you want it or how widely you draw the circle. Here are other advances that I easily could've written about but didn't:

  * PH has finally been [proven infinite](http://arxiv.org/abs/1504.03398) relative to a random oracle
  * We finally understand why computing the [edit distance](http://arxiv.org/abs/1412.0348) between two strings takes nearly-quadratic time
  * Terry Tao [solved the Erdös Discrepancy Problem](http://arxiv.org/abs/1509.05363)
  * The loophole-free Bell test that I [blogged about here](https://scottaaronson.blog/?p=2464) ([Anton Zeilinger](http://edge.org/response-detail/26790) and [Hans Halvorson](http://edge.org/response-detail/26748) discussed this in their _Edge_ answers)
  * Recent progress on the emergence of spacetime from entanglement, and understanding the role of computational complexity in quantum gravity ([Lenny Susskind](http://edge.org/response-detail/26656), [Amanda Gefter](http://edge.org/response-detail/26783), and [Donald Hoffman](http://edge.org/response-detail/26563) all discussed these things in their _Edge_ answers)



I've now read all (more or less) of this year's _Edge_ responses. Even though some of the respondents pushed personal hobbyhorses like I'd feared, I was impressed by how easy it was to discern _themes_ : advances that kept cropping up in one answer after another and that one might therefore guess are actually important (or at least, are currently _perceived_ to be important).

Probably at the top of the list was a new gene-editing technique called CRISPR: [Randolph Neese](http://edge.org/response-detail/26668), [Paul Dolan](http://edge.org/response-detail/26638), [Eric Topol](http://edge.org/response-detail/26683), [Mark Pagel](http://edge.org/response-detail/26605), and [Stuart Firestein](http://edge.org/response-detail/26605) among others all wrote about this, and about its implications for creating designer humans.

Also widely-discussed was the discovery that most psychology studies fail to replicate (I'd long assumed as much, but apparently this was big news in psychology!): [Nicholas Humphrey](http://edge.org/response-detail/26648), [Stephen Kosslyn](http://edge.org/response-detail/26650), [Jonathan Schooler](http://edge.org/response-detail/26751), [Ellen Winner](http://edge.org/response-detail/26695), [Judith Rich Harris](http://edge.org/response-detail/26682), and [Philip Tetlock](http://edge.org/response-detail/26659) all wrote about that.

Then there was the Pluto flyby, which [Juan Enriquez](http://edge.org/response-detail/26595), [Roger Highfield](http://edge.org/response-detail/26580), and [Nicholas Christakis](http://edge.org/response-detail/26740) all wrote about. (As Christakis, Master of Silliman College at Yale, was so recently a victim of a social-justice mob, I found it moving how he simply ignored those baying for his head and turned his attention heavenward in his _Edge_ answer.)

Then there was progress in deep learning, including Google's Deep Dream (those [images of dogs in nebulae](http://d.ibtimes.co.uk/en/full/1447156/nebula-photograph-captured-by-hubble-telescope.jpg?w=762) that filled your Facebook wall) and DeepMind (the program that taught itself how to play dozens of classic video games). [Steve Omohundro](http://edge.org/response-detail/26689), [Andy Clark](http://edge.org/response-detail/26635), [Jamshed Bharucha](http://edge.org/response-detail/26629), [Kevin Kelly](http://edge.org/response-detail/26780), [David Dalrymple](http://edge.org/response-detail/26794), and [Alexander Wissner-Gross](http://edge.org/response-detail/26587) all wrote about different aspects of this story.

And recent progress in SETI, which [Yuri Milner](http://edge.org/response-detail/26739) (who's given $100 million for it) and [Mario Livio](http://edge.org/response-detail/26577) wrote about.

Unsurprisingly, a bunch of high-energy physicists wrote about high-energy physics at the LHC: how the Higgs boson was found (still news?), how nothing _other_ than the Higgs boson was found (the biggest news?), but how there's now the slightest  _hint_ of a new particle at 750 GeV. See [Lee Smolin](http://edge.org/response-detail/26610), [Garrett Lisi](http://edge.org/response-detail/26703), [Sean Carroll](http://edge.org/response-detail/26611), and [Sarah Demers](http://edge.org/response-detail/26732).

Finally, way out on the Pareto frontier of importance and disgustingness was the recently-discovered therapeutic value of transplanting one person's poop into another person's intestines, which [Joichi Ito](http://edge.org/response-detail/26746), [Pamela Rosenkranz](http://edge.org/response-detail/26788), and [Alan Alda](http://edge.org/response-detail/26731) all wrote about (it also, predictably, featured in a recent _South Park_ episode).

Without further ado, here are 27 other answers that struck me in one way or another:

  * [Steven Pinker](http://edge.org/response-detail/26616) on happy happy things are getting better (and we can measure it)
  * [Freeman Dyson](http://edge.org/response-detail/26738) on the Dragonfly astronomical observatory
  * [Jonathan Haidt](http://edge.org/response-detail/26766) on how prejudice against people of differing political opinions was discovered to have surpassed racial, gender, and religious prejudice
  * [S. Abbas Raza](http://edge.org/response-detail/26786) on Piketty's r>g
  * [Rebecca Newberger Goldstein](http://edge.org/response-detail/26654), thoughtful as usual, on the recent study that said it's too simple to say female participation is lower in STEM fields--rather, female participation is lower in all and only those fields, STEM or non-STEM, whose participants believe (rightly or wrongly) that "genius" is required rather than just conscientious effort
  * [Bill Joy](http://edge.org/response-detail/26675) on recent advances on reducing CO2 emissions
  * [Paul Steinhardt](http://edge.org/response-detail/26785) on recent observations saying that, not only were the previous "B-modes from inflation" just galactic dust, but there are _no_ real B-modes to within the current detection limits, and this poses a problem for inflation (I hadn't heard about this last part)
  * [Aubrey de Grey](http://edge.org/response-detail/26701) on new antibiotics that are grown in the soil rather than in lab cultures
  * [John Tooby](http://edge.org/response-detail/26714) on the evolutionary rationale for germline engineering
  * [W. Tecumseh Fitch](http://edge.org/response-detail/26713) on the coming reality of the "Jurassic Park program" (bringing back extinct species through DNA splicing--though probably not dinosaurs, whose DNA is too degraded)
  * [Keith Devlin](http://edge.org/response-detail/26667) on the new prospect of using massive datasets (from MOOCs, for example) to _actually_ figure out how students learn
  * [Richard Muller](http://edge.org/response-detail/26644) on how air pollution in China has become one of the world's worst problems (imagine every child in Beijing being force-fed two packs of cigarettes per day)
  * [Ara Norenzayan](http://edge.org/response-detail/26658) on the demographic trends in religious belief
  * [James Croak](http://edge.org/response-detail/26622) on amazing advances in battery technology (which were news to me)
  * [Buddhini Samarasinghe](http://edge.org/response-detail/26621) on (among other things) the power of aspirin to _possibly prevent cancer_
  * [Todd Sacktor](http://edge.org/response-detail/26619) on a new treatment for Parkinson's
  * [Charles Seife](http://edge.org/response-detail/26764) on the imminent availability of data about pretty much everything in our lives
  * [Susan Blackmore](http://edge.org/response-detail/26777) on "that dress" and what it revealed about the human visual system
  * [Brian Keating](http://edge.org/response-detail/26759) on experiments that should soon tell us the neutrinos' masses (again, I hadn't heard about these)
  * [Michael McCullough](http://edge.org/response-detail/26752) on something called "reproductive religiosity theory," which posits that the central purpose of religions is to enforce social norms around mating and reproduction (for what it's worth, I'd always regarded that as obvious; it's even expounded in the last chapter of _Quantum Computing Since Democritus_)
  * [Greg Cochran](http://edge.org/response-detail/26754) on the origin of Europeans
  * [David Buss](http://edge.org/response-detail/26747) on the "mating crisis among educated women"
  * [Ed Regis](http://edge.org/response-detail/26606) on how high-fat diets are better (except, isn't this the principle behind Atkins, and isn't this pretty old news by now?)
  * [Melanie Swan](http://edge.org/response-detail/26720) on blockchain-based cryptography, such as Bitcoin (though it wasn't entirely clear to me what point Swan was making about it)
  * [Paul Davies](http://edge.org/response-detail/26599) on LIGO getting ready to detect its first gravitational waves
  * [Samuel Arbesman](http://edge.org/response-detail/26597) on how weather prediction has gotten steadily better (rendering our culture's jokes about the perpetually-wrong weatherman outdated, with hardly anyone noticing)
  * [Alison Gopnik](http://edge.org/response-detail/26626) on how the ubiquity of touchscreen devices like the iPad means that toddlers can now master computers, and this is something genuinely new under the sun (I can testify from personal experience that she's onto something)



Then there were three answers for which the "progress" being celebrated, seemed to me to be progress racing faster into WrongVille:

  * [Frank Tipler](http://edge.org/response-detail/26690) on how one can conclude a priori that there must be a Big Crunch to our future (and hence, the arena for Tiplerian theology) in order to prevent the black hole information paradox from arising, all recent cosmological evidence to the contrary be damned.
  * [Ross Anderson](http://edge.org/response-detail/26757) on an exciting conference whose participants aim to replace quantum mechanics with local realistic theories. (Anderson, in particular, is totally wrong that you can get Bell inequality violation from "a combination of local action and global correlation," unless the global correlation goes as far as a 't-Hooft-like superdeterministic conspiracy.)
  * [Gordon Kane](http://edge.org/response-detail/26607) on how the big news is that the LHC should soon see superparticles. (This would actually be fine except that Kane omits the crucial context, that he's been predicting superparticles just around the corner again and again for the past twenty years and they've never shown up)



Finally, two responses by old friends that amused me. The science-fiction writer [Rudy Rucker](http://edge.org/response-detail/26594) just became aware of the discovery of the dark energy back in 1998, and considers that to be exciting scientific news (yes, Rudy, so it was!). And [Michael Vassar](http://edge.org/response-detail/26773) --the Kevin Bacon or Paul Erdös of the rationalist world, the guy who _everyone_ 's connected to somehow--writes something about a global breakdown of economic rationality, $20 bills on the sidewalk getting ignored, that I had trouble understanding (though the fault is probably mine).
