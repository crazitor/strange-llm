Merry Christmas (belatedly)! This year Quanta Claus has brought us eight fascinating final project reports from students in my [6.845 Quantum Complexity Theory](http://stellar.mit.edu/S/course/6/fa14/6.845/) class, covering everything from interactive proofs to query and communication complexity to quantum algorithms to quantum gates (and one project even includes a web-based demo you can try!). Continuing in the tradition of the two [previous](https://scottaaronson.blog/?p=515) [showcases](https://scottaaronson.blog/?p=1181), I'm sharing the reports here; some of these works might also be posted to the arXiv and/or submitted to journals. Thanks so much to the students who volunteered to participate in the showcase, and to _all_ the students for making this such a great class.

  * [On Applications of the Equilibrium Value Method](http://www.scottaaronson.com/showcase3/booth-serena.pdf), by Serena Booth. A survey about Xiaodi Wu's [simpler alternative approach](http://arxiv.org/abs/1004.0264) to proving QIP=PSPACE, and whether it might also yield a proof of QRG=RG=EXP.
  * [Improved Quantum Query Complexity Bounds for Some Graph Problems](http://www.scottaaronson.com/showcase3/dhariwal-prafulla-mayar-vinay.pdf), by Prafulla Dhariwal and Vinay Mayar. Building on Lin and Lin's [striking recent work](http://arxiv.org/abs/1410.0932) on "Vaidman-bomb query complexity," obtains some new results about the quantum query complexity of k-source shortest paths and minimum vertex cover in bipartite graphs.
  * [On Quantum Sieve Approaches to the Lattice Shortest Vector Problem](http://www.scottaaronson.com/showcase3/epelbaum-daniel.pdf), by Daniel Epelbaum. Surveys Greg Kuperberg's [subexponential-time quantum algorithm](http://arxiv.org/abs/quant-ph/0302112) for the dihedral Hidden Subgroup Problem, as well as Oded Regev's [reduction](http://arxiv.org/abs/cs/0304005) of the approximate shortest vector problem to dihedral HSP. Discusses in detail why these two things, combined, do _not_ yield a quantum algorithm for lattice problems that outperforms the best known classical algorithms.
  * [Approximate Degree of AND-OR Trees](http://www.scottaaronson.com/showcase3/kamath-pritish-vasudevan-prashant.pdf), by Pritish Kamath and Prashant Vasudevan. Discusses the wonderful open problem of proving that every AND-OR tree with N leaves, even a highly-unbalanced one, has approximate degree Ω(√N) as a real polynomial. (We know from [Reichardt's seminal work](http://arxiv.org/abs/1005.1601) on quantum query complexity that the degree is O(√N).) Makes some partial progress on this conjecture--e.g., resolves it up to a polylog(N) factor for trees of constant depth.
  * [Infinite Separation of Quantum Information and Communication](http://www.scottaaronson.com/showcase3/koh-dax-liu-ziwen.pdf), by Dax Koh and Zi-Wen Liu. Wrings a further implication out of the [striking recent work](http://arxiv.org/abs/1407.8217) of Perry, Jain, and Oppenheim, about a communication task with infinite quantum/classical separation ("infinite" meaning that one of them is O(1)--o(1) actually--and the other is Ω(N)).
  * [Taming Quantum Amplitudes with Gateset Limitations](http://www.scottaaronson.com/showcase3/rheingans-yoo-ross.pdf), by Ross Rheingans-Yoo. Surveys the issue of doubly-exponentially small probabilities for PostBQP circuits, which I covered recently [on this blog](https://scottaaronson.blog/?p=2072) as well as [on MathOverflow](http://mathoverflow.net/questions/187995/massive-cancellations). Takes an initial step toward resolving the problem of whether π and e are "tame" numbers, by using the concept of irrationality coefficients to show that a constant number of gates with π- and e-like amplitudes can be tolerated in a PostBQP circuit.
  * [Tools for Quantum Circuit Synthesis](http://www.scottaaronson.com/showcase3/voss-chelsea.pdf), by Chelsea Voss. [**Click here to try Chelsea 's software!**](http://quantummechanic.xvm.mit.edu:8000/) Discusses the creation of a web-based tool that lets you experiment with synthesizing quantum circuits and observing their behavior, and also gives you quantum-circuit-synthesis puzzles to solve.
  * [Complexity of the Quantum Separability Problem and Its Variants](http://www.scottaaronson.com/showcase3/xu-charles.pdf), by Charles Xu. Surveys the problem of deciding whether a quantum state described in various ways is separable or far from separable (and the use of that problem to characterize quantum interactive proof classes), concentrating on [recent work](http://arxiv.org/abs/1308.5788) by Gutoski, Hayden, Milner, and Wilde.
