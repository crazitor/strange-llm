## Abstract

This paper argues that the primary risk posed by artificial intelligence is not technical failure but psychological amplification. Human civilization operates through abstractions—such as nation, direction, profession, religion, and status—which function as necessary coordination tools. However, when these conceptual systems become objects of psychological identification, they generate ego-structures rooted in comparison, fear, insecurity, and competition. This condition is described here as human unconsciousness: the automation of thought and identity without reflective awareness. -/- Artificial intelligence, as an optimization system, does not(...)generate values independently; it reflects and amplifies the psychological structures embedded within its creators. If human systems remain conditioned by ego-driven fragmentation, AI will scale these patterns globally. Thus, alignment is not merely a technical challenge but a civilizational and phenomenological one. -/- The paper proposes that AI governance requires inner maturity: the capacity to observe thought without identification and to maintain abstractions as functional tools rather than identities. The future of superintelligence depends not only on engineering safeguards but on the development of psychological awareness. Without this foundation, AI will not transcend human unconsciousness—it will intensify it. (shrink)
