## Abstract

Debates about the moral status of artificial agents often focus on sentience or full personhood, thresholds that are difficult to verify. This paper introduces the concept of “moral patiency candidacy” as a lower and more operational threshold for ethical concern. Patiency candidacy marks the point at which treating an agent purely instrumentally becomes ethically unstable under conditions of uncertainty while setting aside intractable metaphysical questions of personhood. Drawing on precautionary approaches to artificial sentience and relational accounts of moral status, the(...)paper proposes a heuristic rubric for identifying convergence across observable indicators such as reciprocal interaction, communicative participation, and sustained social embedding. These indicators are not treated as proof of moral status but as signals of escalating moral risk that may justify constraints on unilateral control. The rubric can be operationalized through structured deliberative methods, such as Delphi-style expert panels, to support institutional decision-making. By shifting attention from metaphysical classification to governance under risk, the concept of patiency candidacy offers a pragmatic framework for evaluating artificial systems whose social roles may outpace our ability to determine their intrinsic moral standing.
