## Abstract

This work develops a structural account of cognition based on the principle that distinction (∆) is not a product of thought, but a condition for its emergence. It argues that perception, attention, memory, emotion, and learning are not discrete modules but continuous processes of navigating, maintaining, and transforming difference. -/- Central to the theory are several recurrent dynamics: the use of internal predictive structures (frames), the tension produced by conflicting interpretations (Overcell), the reorganization of mental models under stress (Collapse), and(...)the experience of felt discrepancy when the self is implicated in unresolved contradiction (Qualia). These processes do not operate in isolation but loop recursively through attentional constraints (Aperture), drift (uncertain navigation), and recursive modulation (Reflexivity). -/- The resulting picture is not of mind as processor, but as an adaptive structure under tension — one that survives by sensing when its own architecture fails, and by restructuring under pressure. This text offers a description of how systems, human or artificial, navigate breakdown without losing coherence. (shrink)
