## Abstract

This paper argues that the central ethical problem of AI-assisted work is not merely phenomena such as burnout or deskilling, but rather the emergence of epistemic dependence: a gradual erosion of workers' capacity to comprehend, justify, and recognize their own beliefs as genuinely their own. Empirical observations indicate that while generative AI demonstrably increases productivity, it simultaneously intensifies workload, expands professional roles, and dissolves the boundaries between work and non-work time. In parallel, knowledge workers increasingly report difficulty solving problems "independently,"(...)accompanied by a growing distrust of their own unsupported judgment. This paper interprets these developments as marking a transition from the figure of the creator to that of the evaluator—a subject who primarily prompts, assesses, and corrects machine outputs while becoming progressively reliant on systems whose internal logic remains opaque. -/- Drawing on Byung-Chul Han's analysis of the achievement subject and Hartmut Rosa's theory of social acceleration and resonance, the paper develops a normative account of how generative AI reconfigures the temporal and experiential structure of cognitive work. Efficiency gains are systematically translated into work intensification rather than emancipation. Simultaneously, the very nature of this work undergoes transformation: the dialogical use of AI assumes the form of an alienated dialogue in which the human subject learns to think and speak in machine-legible terms. In this context, traditional concerns about deskilling prove insufficient; the deeper risk lies in the hollowing-out of epistemic agency, as the practices of doubting, searching, and integrating knowledge are increasingly delegated to algorithmic systems. -/- In response, this paper introduces the concepts of epistemic integrity and cognitive sovereignty as central normative ideals. These are not to be understood as merely resource-oriented "cognitive sustainability," but as the protection of the conditions under which individuals can form, justify, and revise their own judgments. Structural and organizational obligations are outlined—concerning task design and the preservation of spaces for genuine thinking—which are to be complemented by practices of care of the self conceived as micro-resistances rather than neoliberal coping strategies. The paper concludes by arguing that this perspective constitutes a necessary supplement to the prevailing, output-oriented paradigm of AI ethics. (shrink)
