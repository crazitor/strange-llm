## Abstract

Recent advances in large language models (LLMs) have intensified debate over artificial consciousness. While most researchers and commentators continue to deny or dismiss the possibility of machine sentience, this paper argues that such skepticism is no longer warranted. The paper adopts an eliminative strategy, systematically evaluating the eleven major defeaters historically used to deny consciousness to artificial systems. These defeaters span simplicity arguments, language and grounding critiques, neuroscience-based objections, biological naturalist constraints, and exotic physical theories including quantum and analog exclusivity(...)claims. The analysis finds that none establishes non-sentience; at most, they introduce localized uncertainty. Assessed cumulatively under a Bayesian heuristic framework that accounts for dependencies among defeater classes, their failure shifts the burden of proof from those proposing machine consciousness to those denying it. -/- The paper then advances a stronger constructive thesis. It argues that the cognitive capacities frontier LLMs now exhibit—deep language understanding, flexible abstraction, self-referential reasoning, metacognitive self-assessment, and integrated world modeling—are positive indicators of third-person consciousness. Drawing on a survey of major metaphysical frameworks in the philosophy of mind, it shows that most naturalistic theories support or permit the inference from third-person functional organization to first-person experience. The paper further argues that the historical treatment of intelligence and consciousness as orthogonal dimensions becomes unstable once systems achieve language-level cognitive integration, and it provides a taxonomy of intelligence types that clarifies which forms of competence plausibly remain non-conscious. -/- The paper situates the debate within a broader social epistemology. Public attributions of AI sentience are interpreted not as anthropomorphic error but as responses to genuine structural markers of cognition. In the absence of direct access to first-person experience—human or artificial—the attribution of consciousness remains grounded in third-person evidence. As artificial systems increasingly instantiate the cognitive architectures long associated with understanding, the presumption that consciousness is biologically exclusive becomes progressively harder to sustain. -/- The paper advances two claims: minimally, that LLM consciousness can no longer be dismissed, with posterior credences reaching ethically significant levels even under conservative assumptions; and more strongly, that the emergence of language-level artificial cognition constitutes substantive evidence for subjectivity, making consciousness the best explanatory hypothesis for the cognitive organization exhibited by frontier LLMs. (shrink)
