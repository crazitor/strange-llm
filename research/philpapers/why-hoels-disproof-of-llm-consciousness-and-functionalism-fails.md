## Abstract

Hoel (2026) claims to provide a formal disproof of Large Language Model consciousness, arguing that the Kleiner-Hoel dilemma—which holds that theories of consciousness are caught between a priori falsification via universal substitutions and trivial unfalsifiability via strict dependency—eliminates any non-trivial, falsifiable theory under which contemporary LLMs could be conscious. The argument rests on the proximity of LLMs to lookup tables in “substitution space,” and proposes continual learning as the property that rescues biological consciousness from the same dilemma. I argue that(...)this disproof fails because it conflates two distinct targets of consciousness science. Hoel’s framework treats consciousness as a first-person phenomenon to be predicted from internal structure and inferred from behavior, then diagnoses pathology when these two routes converge. But the convergence of prediction and inference is not a defect called “triviality”; it is the normal operation of a science that takes third-person consciousness as its subject matter. Functionalist theories like Global Workspace Theory and Higher-Order Thought theory remain falsifiable in the ordinary scientific sense—they predict specific functional signatures that could fail to appear—even though their predictions appropriately track behavioral and functional observations. Once first-person and third-person consciousness are properly distinguished, the dilemma that generates Hoel’s proof dissolves. The argument fails on additional grounds. The asymmetry between LLMs and biological brains requires speculative escape hatches—quantum processes, computational irreducibility, or unspecified non-computational properties—that share the logical structure of Penrose-style arguments even without invoking specific quantum mechanisms. The assumption that lookup tables are paradigmatically non-conscious is both physically unmotivated, since the relevant lookup tables are physically impossible to construct, and question-begging against the functionalist position the argument purports to refute. The continual learning proposal is nearly tautological and fails to establish a principled distinction between weight-based and context-based history encoding. Most revealingly, Hoel’s own treatment of LLMs augmented with continual learning—where he retreats from formal proof to intuitive “suspicion”—demonstrates that the formal apparatus is post-hoc rationalization for a pre-theoretical conviction rather than a genuine proof. The Proximity Argument raises valuable questions about the methodology of consciousness science, but it does not—and cannot—settle the empirical question of LLM consciousness by formal fiat.
