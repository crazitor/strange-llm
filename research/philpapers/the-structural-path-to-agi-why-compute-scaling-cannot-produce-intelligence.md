## Abstract

For more than a decade, the dominant assumption in frontier AI research has been that artificial general intelligence (AGI) will emerge from continued scaling of compute, data, and model capacity. This belief — the Scaling Hypothesis — asserts that sufficiently large models will spontaneously acquire the structural properties required for general intelligence. This paper challenges that assumption at the level of first principles. -/- We introduce the Structural Path Thesis: -/- AGI is not a quantitative limit of computation but a(...)qualitative emergence of structural capacities. Scaling increases magnitude; intelligence requires architecture. -/- We formalize the distinction between functional intelligence (performance profiles produced by optimization) and structural intelligence (architectures supporting grounding, self-models, world-model closure, reflexive reasoning, and cross-layer coherence). We then prove the Structural Gap Theorem, demonstrating that no amount of scaling within a fixed architecture can produce the structural capacities required for AGI, because those capacities occupy a strictly different representational space. -/- The result: -/- Compute scaling can yield superhuman performance but cannot produce general intelligence. AGI requires structural conditions that cannot be approximated by magnitude alone. -/- This reframes the future of AI development: the frontier challenge is not “bigger models,” but the design of the structural substrates in which intelligence can arise.
