## Abstract

Start here (base logic + reading order): Free Will Without Metaphysical Magic (++++ ≠ +++=) Artificial systems increasingly perform evaluative functions that rival or exceed human capacities: forecasting outcomes, ranking options, and optimising across vast informational domains. This paper argues that none of these capabilities constitute agency in the morally relevant sense. Evaluation is not agency. Agency requires authorised resolution under exposure to consequence—the binding of a system to a trajectory it cannot exit, such that the same continuous subject bears(...)the downstream costs of its commitments across time. Artificial systems can be retrained, reset, replaced, or insulated from consequence by design; any apparent “decision” therefore remains authored upstream by the human or institutional sponsors who authorise deployment. Responsibility cannot migrate with capability, and “human-in-the-loop” arrangements do not dissolve authorship when authority is retained. Speculation about artificial consciousness does not alter this structural account: where genuine stake is credibly suspected, precautionary constraints may be warranted, but responsibility remains non-delegable. The paper concludes that reckless pursuit of artificial emergence without enforceable guardrails constitutes a severe moral hazard and should be treated as a prohibited risk class under both moral and legal reasoning.
