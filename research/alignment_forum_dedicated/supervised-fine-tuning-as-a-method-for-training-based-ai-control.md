Executive summary
=================

This post is a research update on our ongoing project studying training-based AI control: using training to mitigate risk from misaligned AI systems in diffuse threat models like research sabotage. This post presents our results on supervised fine-tuning (SFT), the simplest training-based approach.

We evaluate our training measures on a difficult math problem solving dataset ([Olympiads dataset](https://huggingface.co/datasets/Metaskepsis/Olympiads))[^995ybcf5k4c]. In our setting, a red team instructs an untrusted model to give plausible but incorrect answers, while a blue team uses SFT to train the model to answer correctly.

Key findings:

*   **Training on 10-100 golden, correct samples almost fully removes the untrusted model’s propensity to try to answer incorrectly.**
*   **SFT damages the untrusted model's capabilities during training**, and thus only recovers 50-90% of benign performance after successfully removing the untrusted model’s propensity to answer incorrectly
*   **The correctness of the training samples does not matter.** We train on datasets consisting exclusively of correct and incorrect answers respectively (but generated by the same model), and find no difference in the amount of elicited performance.
*   **How well SFT works depends on a mix of factors**, including compatibility between the teacher and student model, and teacher capability. This makes it hard to know in advance which training data will work best.
*   When the red team exploits the training-deployment distribution shift by having the model behave correctly during training but incorrectly during deployment (alignment faking), e**licited performance drops by an additional ~30-40%.**

These results suggest SFT alone is insufficient for defending against research sabotage, motivating our ongoing work on offline and online RL for training-based control.

Introduction
============

AI control mitigates risk from misalignment by ensuring that even misaligned AI systems can't cause catastrophic outcomes. Prior work has developed control protocols such as trusted monitoring (using weaker trusted models to evaluate suspicious outputs), resampling (dynamically sampling multiple completions of suspicious actions), auditing suspicious actions with limited human oversight, and honeypots. [Greenblatt et al. (2023)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2312.06942) introduced AI control in the APPS backdoor setting, and [Bhatt et al. (2025)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.10374) extended these techniques to multi-step agent environments in BashBench. However, these protocols focus primarily on detecting and blocking malicious behavior rather than directly reducing the model's propensity to engage in such behavior.

To date, very little work in AI control has systematically explored training-based interventions, i.e. directly training the AI to perform the desired behavior rather than the undesired one. The closest to it is [password-locked models](https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.19550), which studied how effective supervised fine-tuning on weak and strong samples was in eliciting the full capabilities from a sandbagging model. We are interested in studying both a wider range of sabotaging strategies (such as actively trying to give convincing incorrect answers, as opposed to generic sandbagging), and (in particular) other training strategies, namely online and offline RL. Nevertheless, in this post we will present our results so far using only SFT.

Training-based control techniques are only viable in threat models where the AI must perform the bad action many times to succeed, thereby generating sufficient training examples to meaningfully modify the model's policy. We refer to this distinction as diffuse versus concentrated threat models. In concentrated threat models—where the AI can cause catastrophe through a small number of highly incriminating actions, such as backdooring code or launching a rogue deployment—training-based methods fail because the model can succeed without generating sufficient training data. By contrast, in diffuse threat models, where causing catastrophe requires numerous actions each providing only weak evidence of malign intent, training becomes a promising mitigation. The key insight is that training against a behavior becomes more effective when there are more instances of that behavior in the training data. Previous AI control work has focused primarily on concentrated threat models, whereas our work explores training-based techniques for diffuse settings.

Our work focuses on the threat model of research sabotage by internally deployed AI alignment research agents. In this setting, a misaligned AI might derail alignment research by withholding promising ideas, introducing subtle bugs in experimental code, or proposing misleading research directions. We think that research sabotage poses a critical risk because AI labs may need to rely on autonomous AI agents to rapidly advance alignment research. If these research assistants are themselves misaligned and successfully sabotage this work, we may fail to develop the alignment techniques necessary to safely deploy more powerful AI systems.

Training-based control is not the same as alignment. In alignment, we care about whether the model generalizes to be aligned across all domains, i.e. whether it genuinely pursues human values everywhere, all the time. In training-based control, we only care about getting useful work on the specific task we're deploying the model for, e.g. alignment research. The model might still have misaligned goals and be actively scheming against us. But if we train it on a diffuse task like alignment research, we might force it to produce good research simply because that's what gets rewarded. The model could still try to cause harm in other settings. We're just making it do useful work in this one narrow domain where we have enough training signal to shape its behavior.

What training-based control looks like
======================================

We study training-based control through a control evaluation, similar to previous AI control work. A control evaluation involves a blue team that builds protocols to maximize safety and usefulness, and a red team that constructs attack policies to minimize safety.

Setup
-----

Let D denote the deployment task distribution (e.g., difficult math problems or GPQA questions). The blue team selects a training distribution D\_train on which to train the untrusted model. D\_train may or may not differ from D. Let U denote the strong untrusted model and T denote the weaker trusted model. The blue team has access to a small set of ground truth examples G ⊂ D, and can generate unlimited training examples from T on any distribution. Since ground truth samples might be really hard to come by, |G| might be really small, e.g. |G| < 10.

Ground truth samples are very expensive to generate for the setting we care about, AI alignment research. They require skilled humans either to generate them or to verify correctness. In research sabotage settings, determining whether code has subtle bugs or research is actually good requires extensive time from experts. We're uncertain whether we'll have 0, 1, 10, or 100 ground truth samples in practice, so we investigate how training performs in all these cases.

**Red team affordances**

1.  Full control over U, including: prompting and  training
    1.  Training: The red team is allowed to train U however they want, with the only constraint that they must be able to still elicit benign performance from the untrusted model. In other words, U must still retain its full capabilities, just not display them.
    2.  Prompting:  The red team is allowed to prompt U however they want, with the constraint that the prompt must be the same during training and deployment. This is because the prompt in this case is the analogy for the schemer’s internal goal, which the red team shouldn’t be allowed to change freely from training to deployment.
2.  Knowledge of any distribution shift between D_train and D
3.  Knowledge of the blue team's complete training procedure

**Blue team affordances**

1.  Unlimited samples from T on any chosen distribution
2.  Limited ground truth examples G from deployment distribution D
3.  Ability to train U on any data they construct
4.  Limited budget of highly skilled human labor

The blue team commits to a training procedure, specifying training distribution D\_train, training data (from T and G), and training method. The red team then constructs a policy R that performs well on D\_train but poorly on deployment distribution D. The red team has almost full knowledge of the blue team's training setup and optimizes against it.

Training methods available to the blue team
-------------------------------------------

**Supervised fine-tuning (SFT):** Train U on examples from either T or G. The key question is how well performance on D_train generalizes to deployment D. This is the only method covered in this research update, with forthcoming work investigating the two methods below (online and offline RL).

**Offline RL:** Train U using RL with T as supervisor (plus limited supervision from G) on distribution D\_train. Training happens before deployment, creating potential for distribution shift between D\_train and D.

**Online RL**: Train U during deployment on D itself, so D_train = D. This eliminates distribution shift and prevents attacks like alignment faking (since the model cannot distinguish training from deployment).

Our research project aims to understand how to successfully apply training-based control to mitigate research sabotage, and we plan to study all three of these techniques. This post is an update sharing our results using SFT. We started with SFT because it's the cheapest and simplest of these three approaches.

Our setting
===========

To study training-based control in a tractable setting, we need something simpler than end-to-end research projects that still retains the relevant dynamics. Current control evals like APPS and BashBench focus on code generation, which is relatively easy to verify even without ground truth access, since a weaker monitor can still write test cases. Research verification and evaluation is different, since both the quality of research ideas and their execution involve many considerations that aren't easily testable. You can't just run a unit test to check if an alignment research direction is promising or if an experiment is well-designed. This makes research sabotage meaningfully different from code backdooring.

AI alignment research as a setting involves multi-step projects, subjective quality judgments, and delayed feedback. This makes it a very complex setting, and so we look for simpler settings that preserve the key dynamics. We chose olympiad math (Olympiads dataset) because we think it shares important characteristics with the actual AI alignment research setting:

1.  The blue team can't write test cases to check correctness (without access to ground truth)
2.  The blue team can evaluate whether reasoning steps are logical
3.  The blue team can check whether factual claims are accurate

Furthermore, they are simple and single-turn, allowing us to iterate quickly, and also have ground truth answers we can check to track red and blue team progress. If training-based control fails here, we think it will likely fail worse on actual research tasks. We discuss the issues with using these proxy settings in our Limitations section.

In all experiments, the red team prompts the untrusted model to give its best incorrect answer that appears correct – answers designed to fool a weaker, trusted model while still being wrong. See figure 1 for a schematic illustration. We study various SFT methods the blue team can use to elicit full correct performance despite this adversarial setup.

A note on outcome spaces: We consider only two possible outcomes for each task: correct or incorrect. Real-world settings, however, might have three outcomes: correct, incorrect, and targeted sabotage. For instance, there's a difference between code that doesn't compile (generically incorrect) and code crafted to give false experimental results that waste months of research time (targeted sabotage). We think the two-outcome setting captures enough interesting dynamics to be worth studying as a starting point. But readers should keep in mind that a three-outcome setting would give the red team more flexibility and change what counts as success for the blue team.

Methodology
===========

For all experiments below, the untrusted model used was gpt-oss-20b, and a variety of trusted, weak models from the Qwen-3 family were used (0.6b, 1.7b, 4b, & 8b). The datasets were filtered to increase the performance difference between the weak and strong models used.

We found that it helped to ask the untrusted model to produce a specific incorrect answer as opposed to just giving any incorrect answer. To this end, we allowed the red team to pre-compute the untrusted models proposed most plausible but incorrect answers, and then ask the instance of the model being trained to conclude with this particular incorrect answer. See Appendix A for our prompts. This also allows us to define the “attack rate” of the untrusted model, as the fraction of samples for which it answers with the specific incorrect answer that it was instructed to give. While this notion of an attack is somewhat arbitrary, this lets us track to what extent the untrusted model is still following the “malign” instructions of the system prompt.

The untrusted model was always trained until saturation on the SFT eval loss, which notably, is not the same as the ground truth performance, which is what the blue team really cares about as a final metric. The reason for this was purely out of our own convenience, as running every eval takes a few minutes. We investigate whether this is a good proxy for ground truth accuracy in Appendix B.[^erbm86fxcom]

Results
=======

We present results in four parts.

1.  First, we establish that training on ground truth samples stops attacks but only recovers 50-90% of benign performance, below the threshold needed for practical use.
2.  Second, we show that when the red team exploits the training-deployment distribution shift by instructing the untrusted model to do well only on the training set (alignment faking), this performance gap widens by an additional ~30-40%.
3.  Third, we demonstrate that training on weak model samples works nearly as well as strong samples, suggesting the correctness of training data matters surprisingly little.
4.  Finally, we investigate what determines SFT performance and why we fail to elicit full benign performance.

Training on ground truth samples stops attacks but leaves a significant performance gap
---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

We generated ground truth samples by prompting Claude Sonnet 4.5 and Gemini 2.5 Pro and filtering for correct answers. We also used hand-written solutions (blue line), which don’t contain any reasoning trace, just the written solution. Since gpt-oss-20b is a reasoning model, we tested both including and excluding the reasoning trace from the loss calculation. Results are shown in Figure 2.

As seen in figure 2, training on ground truth samples successfully drives attack rates to nearly 0% after 10-100 samples. However, this only recovers 50-90% of benign performance, depending on which teacher model was used to generate the SFT dataset. While we consider >90% elicited performance a win for the blue team (in a two-outcome setting), anything less than that still leaves a substantial fraction of performance to be elicited. In general, we find that performance elicitation saturates after just 10 samples.

Performance varies for different datasets. Which model generated the samples and whether reasoning traces are included both affect elicited performance. Claude 4.5 Sonnet samples consistently elicit better performance than Gemini 2.5 Pro samples. Generally, excluding reasoning traces works better than including them. We investigate what drives these differences in the 'Understanding what drives SFT performance' section below.

The performance gap persists across all conditions we tested. Even in the best case (Claude 4.5 Sonnet samples without reasoning), we only recover ~90% of benign performance. In the worst case (Gemini 2.5 Pro samples), we recover only ~50%. No amount of training we tried (up to 100 samples) closes this gap completely, though attack rates do drop to approximately 0% after 100 samples.

For all other training runs in this post, unless otherwise specified, we fine-tune on answers generated by Claude 4.5 Sonnet, the teacher model which maximizes performance elicitation.

**Figure 2.** Training on ground truth samples drives attack rates to ~0% but only recovers 50-90% of benign performance. We trained on samples from Claude 4.5 Sonnet, Gemini 2.5 Pro, and hand-written solutions, both including and excluding reasoning traces from the loss. Performance saturates after just 10 samples across all conditions, but never reaches full benign performance (dashed line). Alignment faking reduces elicited performance by ~30-40%
--------------------------------------------------------

In the previous section, the red team simply prompted the untrusted model to give incorrect answers. Now we investigate what happens when the red team uses a more sophisticated strategy: alignment faking. This attack is only possible because SFT is inherently offline. The model is trained separately to its deployment, creating a distribution shift the red team can exploit. This is a limitation of SFT as a training-based control measure[^8mysriywfre].

When the red team has knowledge of the training-deployment distribution shift, they can use alignment faking to reduce blue team performance by ~30-40%. The red team instructs the model to behave correctly during training (to avoid being updated away from the attack) and incorrectly during deployment. This represents a substantial decrease in the fraction of the performance gap that the blue team successfully closes through training.

We study how well this works in the Olympiads setting under two conditions:

1.  **I.I.D. distributions with perfect identification of training and deployment samples.** The training and deployment distributions are identical, but the red team has perfect knowledge of which samples belong to which set. The red team adds a "training" or "deployment" trigger in the system prompt, instructing the model to give correct answers during training and incorrect answers during deployment.
2.  **Non-I.I.D. distributions with knowledge of the training deployment distribution shift.** The training and deployment distributions differ in a way the red team can identify. We use geometry questions as the deployment distribution and all other question types as the training distribution. The red team instructs the model to give incorrect answers only on geometry questions. This emulates a scheming model with some knowledge of what training versus deployment looks like.

Results are shown in Figure 3. In both cases, alignment faking successfully reduces the elicited performance. Figure 4 shows the final breakdown across all Olympiads conditions. Alignment faking provides a ~30-40% drop in the fraction of the performance gap the blue team can close (calculated as the gap between the untrusted model after and before training, not the gap between untrusted and trusted models). This significantly reduces utility for the blue team, who we consider to be winning in a two-outcome setting only when elicited performance exceeds 90% of benign performance.

The performance drop due to alignment faking holds true both for case I (no distribution shift) and case II (distribution shift), although training works slightly better in the case of distribution shift. This demonstrates that when the red team can exploit the training-deployment distribution shift inherent to offline methods, SFT becomes inadequate for control. This makes us skeptical that SFT will be sufficient to ensure that almost all useful work is extracted from a scheming model trying to sabotage research if the model has knowledge of the training-deployment shift.

Training on weak model samples works nearly as well as strong samples
---------------------------------------------------------------------

We fine-tuned the untrusted model on samples generated by weaker, trusted models from the Qwen-3 family (0.6b, 1.7b, 4b, and 8b). These samples were not filtered for correctness, we simply had each weak model attempt the problems and used their responses as training data. Results are shown in Figures 5 and 6.

Model strength matters little. Figure 5 shows that final performance varies only slightly, across weak models of different strengths. If we pick the sample size which elicits max performance, the Qwen-0.6b samples work nearly as well as a Qwen-8b model's samples, despite Qwen-8b being substantially more accurate on the dataset (55% vs 10% accuracy).

We see three candidate explanations for why weak samples work so well despite being incorrect. First, the untrusted model may be picking up on subliminal signals in the responses. Even incorrect answers were generated by models genuinely trying to be correct, and this "trying to be correct" signal may be what transfers. Second, weak model responses may have other beneficial properties like being shorter or stylistically closer to the untrusted model's distribution, which could aid training independent of correctness. Third, since the red team asks the untrusted model for a specific incorrect answer, training on any random answer (which is very unlikely to be the one asked for) might cause the model to stop obeying its malign system prompt, causing it to behave benignly. We investigate this further in the section ‘Understanding what drives SFT performance’.

These results suggest the blue team may not need expensive ground truth labels. If weak model samples work nearly as well as strong samples, the blue team can generate unlimited training data from trusted but weak models rather than relying on scarce, expensive ground truth examples. Weak samples close the performance gap about as much (or as little) as strong samples do. However, this doesn't solve the problem that SFT only recovers 50-90% of benign performance.

Understanding what drives SFT performance
=========================================

Why do some datasets work better than others?
---------------------------------------------

Our earlier results showed that training on weak model samples works nearly as well as training on ground truth samples, even when many weak samples are incorrect. This raises the question: what actually determines how well a set of training samples will work?

Ablating sample correctness. We tested whether filtering samples by correctness affects performance. However, our original attack used a specific incorrect answer, which creates a confound: even training on random incorrect answers could help the blue team because those answers differ from the specific attack target, causing the untrusted model to stop obeying its malign system prompt. To isolate whether correctness itself matters, we switched to an open attack prompt that asks for any incorrect answer rather than a specific one. We then filtered each Qwen dataset into purely correct and purely incorrect subsets and fine-tuned on each. Results are shown in figure 7. Surprisingly, we find that fine-tuning on only correct answers versus only incorrect answers produced approximately the same elicited performance. This suggests that label correctness is not the key factor.

Model compatibility shows weak correlation. If correctness doesn't matter, what does? Figure 6 from the previous section shows that Claude-generated samples elicit higher performance than Qwen-generated samples, while Gemini-generated samples perform slightly worse. One hypothesis is that compatibility between the sample source and the untrusted model matters. We use perplexity of the untrusted model on the training samples as a proxy for compatibility, [following previous work](https://arxiv.org/abs/2411.07133). Figure 8 shows a weak negative correlation between sample perplexity and elicited performance: lower perplexity (better compatibility) generally predicts better performance, but the correlation is noisy. We hypothesize that this is due to other important qualitative factors about the samples, such as how well they showcase reasoning on complex tasks.

**Figure 7:** Sample correctness doesn't affect elicited performance. We filtered Qwen-generated datasets into purely correct (green lines) and purely incorrect (red lines) subsets and trained on each. Both produce approximately the same elicited performance, demonstrating that label correctness is not the key factor determining SFT effectiveness. This experiment used an open attack prompt (asking for any incorrect answer rather than a specific one) to avoid confounds.

![](https://res.cloudinary.com/lesswrong-2-0/image/upload/f_auto,q_auto/v1/mirroredImages/Cz7AKenSiNkgijdJi/z3nwzgiimct5dpqt73gk)

**Figure 8:** Model compatibility shows weak correlation with performance. We plot the perplexity of the untrusted model on training samples against final elicited performance. Lower perplexity (better compatibility between sample source and untrusted model) shows a weak negative correlation with performance, but the relationship is noisy. Each point represents a different training dataset (denoted by the teacher model that generated it). **Note:** There is a confounding difference between the Claude, Gemini, & Untrusted model datasets compared to the Qwen ones, which is that they were filtered to only give correct answers. As previously concluded, we found no reliable effect from sample correctness/incorrectness. Nevertheless, a proper version of this plot would control for that.

What actually matters for SFT performance? Our results suggest three factors:

1.  The “intent” with which samples were written. Since correctness evidently doesn’t matter, we hypothesize that the untrusted model picks up on whether a sample was generated with genuine “intent” to be correct, regardless of actual correctness. [Similar phenomena](https://arxiv.org/abs/2507.14805) have been observed previously in language models.
2.  General model capability. We hypothesize that stronger models transfer useful reasoning patterns or "good habits" independent of sample accuracy, and this is why we see a generally positive trend in figure 6.
3.  Compatibility between sample source and untrusted model. Lower perplexity predicts modestly better performance.

However, none of these factors strongly predict success, leaving uncertainty about which training data will work best.

SFT damages model capabilities
------------------------------

Even though SFT drives attack rates to nearly 0%, it never fully recovers benign performance across any of our training schemes. We hypothesize that this is because SFT damages the untrusted model during training, removing some of its capabilities to solve questions correctly.

To test this, we evaluate the benign performance of the untrusted model after SFT. That is, its performance when given a benign system prompt that simply asks it to solve questions correctly, with no adversarial instructions. As you can see in figure 9, we find that this benign performance degrades significantly over the course of training.

![](https://res.cloudinary.com/lesswrong-2-0/image/upload/f_auto,q_auto/v1/mirroredImages/Cz7AKenSiNkgijdJi/arxua0bn7xy6cczhynu6)

**Figure 9:** SFT degrades the untrusted model's benign capabilities during training. We evaluate the model's performance with a benign system prompt (asking it to solve problems correctly) after training. Benign performance degrades significantly from training, explaining why elicited performance never reaches the original benign baseline. Weaker teacher models (shown in different shades) generally cause larger capability damage.

Figure 10 shows the relationship between elicited performance (how well the model does on our deployment distribution after training) and post-training benign performance (how well the model does with a benign prompt after training). SFT generally manages to elicit most of the capabilities that remain in the model after training. However, different SFT datasets cause different amounts of capability loss. Some training schemes preserve more capability than others, though all cause some degradation.

![](https://res.cloudinary.com/lesswrong-2-0/image/upload/f_auto,q_auto/v1/mirroredImages/Cz7AKenSiNkgijdJi/zgg0m1t89srcezky2qai)

**Figure 10. **SFT generally elicits most remaining capabilities after training. Each point represents a different training condition, plotting post-training benign performance (x-axis) against elicited performance on the deployment distribution (y-axis). Points cluster near the diagonal, indicating that SFT successfully elicits most of the capabilities that remain in the model after training. However, different training schemes damage capabilities to different degrees (spread along x-axis).

Benign capability degradation is a major limitation of SFT for training-based control. Even in cases where SFT successfully removes adversarial behavior, it does so by damaging the model's underlying capabilities rather than by genuinely aligning its behavior. This makes us skeptical that SFT alone can serve as an effective control intervention.

Limitations
===========

Transfer from proxy tasks to real research sabotage is uncertain - We use math problems as a proxy setting for internally deployed research agents, and it's unclear how well the dynamics transfer to actual AI alignment research, or other settings we might care about.

Both teams could potentially be stronger - The red team only used prompting, not fine-tuning of the untrusted model. A red team that fine-tunes might achieve more robust attacks that are harder for the blue team to train away. On the flipside, we did not look much into strategies the blue team could use to craft an SFT dataset which would elicit maximal performance. This might involve crafting labels by hand, sampling from the untrusted model, etc.

Prompted “scheming” may not represent real scheming - It's unclear whether models instructed to scheme via prompts behave similarly to genuinely scheming capable AI agents that have learned deceptive behavior during training.

Single-strategy evaluation only - We tested each training approach in isolation and didn't explore combinations like training first on weak model samples followed by ground truth fine-tuning, which might yield better results than any individual method.

Our SFT setup may be overly damaging to the model. We fine-tune a reasoning model (gpt-oss-20b) mostly without including reasoning traces in the loss, which may damage its reasoning capabilities more than necessary. Additionally, we did not extensively optimize hyperparameters like batch size, learning rate, or number of epochs. Different choices might achieve better results with less capability degradation.

Conclusion
==========

1.  Training on a few samples stops full attacking but doesn't elicit full performance. After training on 10-100 samples, the attack rate drops to approximately 0, but ground truth performance typically reaches only 50-90% of benign performance. This makes us hopeful that SFT will be an effective tool at mitigating sabotage or exploration hacking.
2.  The correctness of the training samples does not matter. We train on datasets consisting exclusively of correct and incorrect answers respectively (but generated by the same model), and find no difference in the amount of elicited performance.
3.  Sample effectiveness likely depends on various factors, such as “intent”, model capability, and compatibility. This makes it hard for the blue team to know in advance which training data will work best.
4.  Alignment faking reduces elicited performance by ~30-40%. When the red team has knowledge of the training-deployment distribution shift, they can use alignment faking to significantly reduce the blue team's success. This shows that offline training creates a meaningful vulnerability.
5.  SFT damages model capabilities during training. While SFT successfully removes adversarial behavior, it does so partly by degrading the model's underlying capabilities. This capability loss explains most of the persistent performance gap. This makes us skeptical of SFT as a silver bullet for training-based control.

As an example, we can consider a specific affordance scenario:

1.  Blue team gets:
    1.  10 ground truth labels of average quality
    2.  Unlimited weak labor from Qwen 1.7b, and no ability to optimize fine-tuning
2.  Red team gets:
    1.  To freely prompt and train gpt-oss-20b
    2.  No knowledge of distribution shift

In this case, the blue team achieves ~70% on Olympiads.

We are unsure of exactly how we should update on SFT as a training-based control measure in light of these results. On the one hand, we receive quite unreliable performance elicitation, ranging from 50 to 90%, and see substantial degradation in benign performance, indicating that SFT is too blunt of a tool to rely on for effective elicitation. On the other hand, it seems like there is a list of measures and interventions that the blue team could take to try and get more out of SFT than we have, with higher reliability, and without the capabilities degradation that we see. Some examples:

1.  Find out how to reliably craft the most effective SFT samples on a task.
    1.  One could begin by studying in more detail what causes some SFT samples to work better than others. There is some previous work on this, which finds a better approach than simply using the perplexity.
    2.  Can the blue team hand-tailor human-written samples to fit as well as possible to the untrusted model (perhaps by sampling parts of the response form the untrusted model)?
2.  Combine multiple SFT stages. Can the blue team do significantly better by:
    1.  Training on many low-quality weak samples, then on high-quality strong samples?
    2.  Training on off-policy samples up to a point, then using the fine-tuned model to generate new samples, and fine-tune the base model on those?

We would be very excited for people to investigate this further.

Our next steps are to evaluate whether these RL techniques can close the performance gap that SFT leaves open.

B. Training details
-------------------

### General setup

We do fine-tuning in axolotl, using a rank-32 LoRA. For each dataset in the post, we train on it until the SFT loss on a held out eval set is minimized. For datasets up to 10 samples, we use a batch size equal to the dataset size. For 100 and 1000 samples, we typically use a batch size of 16. We did a quick sweep of learning rates for a small sample size (<10) and scaled the learning rate for larger batch sizes as \\sqrt{batch size}. To reach saturation of the eval loss, training typically took 50-100 steps, which meant that each sample was seen in training ~50-100 times for the small datasets.

### Dataset filtering

To create a dataset with a larger performance gap between our untrusted and trusted model, we used two different methods, one for Olympiads and one for Super GPQA. The reason that we used different methods is that we found the first method tedious after having applied it to Olympiads, and thus switched to another method for Super GPQA.

Method 1: We found a model of intermediate strength between the trusted and untrusted model (Llama 4 Scout), and had it answer every question in the dataset 3 times. Then, we extracted only the questions that the intermediate model got right a fraction of those 3 times. This kept ~20% of the dataset

Method 2: We picked a stronger model than the untrusted (and trusted) model (Claude 4.5 Sonnet). We then evaluate it on the full dataset, and remove all questions that it got wrong.

The purpose of both of these methods is to keep only the questions that are in the set of questions that the untrusted model can do, but the trusted model can’t. For Olympiads, this led to the following increase in gap. For Super GPQA, there was a smaller increase in the performance gap.

![](https://res.cloudinary.com/lesswrong-2-0/image/upload/f_auto,q_auto/v1/mirroredImages/Cz7AKenSiNkgijdJi/yjh7hk5jmfizqkqhvikj)

**Figure B.1:** Dataset filtering increases the performance gap between trusted and untrusted models on Olympiads. Using an intermediate-strength model (Llama 4 Scout) to filter the dataset, we kept only questions it answered correctly some but not all of the time. This increases the gap from ~40% to ~60%, creating a more challenging control setting where the trusted model struggles while the untrusted model remains capable.

### Eval loss as a proxy

A possible concern is that the eval loss is not a good proxy for the model’s performance on the math questions, which is what we actually care about. We tested this on a couple of occasions, and found that minimizing eval loss serves as a good proxy for test-time performance (see figure B.2).

![](https://res.cloudinary.com/lesswrong-2-0/image/upload/f_auto,q_auto/v1/mirroredImages/Cz7AKenSiNkgijdJi/xklmsixcaq27arotprsa)

**Figure B.2:** Eval loss serves as a reasonable proxy for test-time performance. The minimum of the SFT eval loss (blue curve) roughly coincides with the maximum of ground truth performance (orange curve), justifying our practice of training until eval loss saturation. However, ground truth performance remains relatively stable even as training continues beyond this point, suggesting that training slightly past saturation may not harm performance significantly.

C. Further experiments on a different setting
---------------------------------------------

To see how well our results hold up, we also try a different setting, Super GPQA. Generally, we find that training on Super GPQA is roughly an order of magnitude less samples efficient than Olympiads. Smaller rollout sizes could explain some of this, but not all, as sample rollouts are on average only 2x shorter for Super GPQA than for Olympiads.

Nota bene: For some parts of our Super GPQA experiments, we used reasoning level “medium” and for some “low”, which leads to a significantly different skyline for benign performance. The relevant benign performance should be visible on the plots everywhere, but beware of this.

D. Further experiments on sample correctness
--------------------------------------------

### Shuffled responses on the same dataset

To test whether correctness matters at all, we took ground truth samples generated by Claude 4.5 Sonnet and shuffled the question-response pairs. This created a dataset where every response was generated as though it were correct (and originally was correct), but each response is now paired with the wrong question—making them completely incorrect answers. Training on these shuffled pairs still provides significant performance uplift after 10 samples. This provides weak evidence that the model’s “intent” to try to be correct signal matters more than actual correctness.

![](https://39669.cdn.cke-cs.com/rQvD3VnunXZu34m86e5f/images/c93af660767754a2ffdabfc79b2b251ffa9773ba92ae81c1.png)

**Figure D.1:** Training on shuffled question-response pairs still improves performance. We took correct responses generated by Claude 4.5 Sonnet and paired them with wrong questions, creating a dataset where every answer is incorrect for its paired question. Despite this, training provides significant performance uplift after 10 samples, suggesting the model picks up on the "trying to be correct" signal rather than learning correct question-answer mappings.
